A pharmaceutical launch does not begin when stock arrives in the warehouse. It begins when the company chooses the product.

By the time a launch team is discussing campaign timing, most of the important decisions have already been made: the regulatory route, manufacturer, batch size, pack, price, supply model and channel strategy. If those decisions were made independently, launch becomes an attempt to solve structural problems with commercial activity.

The route to market should therefore be designed before development and registration commitments become difficult to reverse.

## Product selection shapes everything that follows

The first question is not whether a molecule has demand. It is whether the exact product opportunity can support a viable route.

That means examining:

- dosage form and strength;
- reference product and regulatory pathway;
- development or sourcing requirement;
- competitive intensity;
- market price and likely margin;
- pack size and presentation;
- expected volume;
- manufacturing complexity;
- supply continuity;
- channel behaviour;
- working-capital requirement.

A product with attractive demand but poor batch economics may be weaker than a smaller opportunity with a more suitable manufacturing and supply model.

The best portfolio decisions consider market need and execution difficulty together.

## The regulatory pathway is a commercial decision

Different routes create different timelines, evidence requirements, costs and lifecycle obligations.

A development-led product may require formulation work, analytical transfer, stability, submission batches and validation. A sourced product may depend on availability, licensing route, packaging adaptation and continuity of the source market. A parallel import opportunity is product-specific and commercially sensitive to price movement, pack differences and supply.

The route determines when capital is committed and when revenue can realistically begin. It also influences which partners are required and which risks remain after approval.

Regulatory planning should therefore sit beside the financial model from the start.

## Manufacturing decides whether the plan can scale

Manufacturing strategy is often reduced to unit cost. That is too narrow.

The company needs to understand whether the site can support:

- the dosage form and process;
- development and analytical work;
- the target market’s documentation requirements;
- the required submission and validation batches;
- the proposed pack configuration;
- the commercial batch size;
- capacity at launch and after growth;
- change control and lifecycle support.

A low quoted cost is not useful if the minimum batch creates three years of inventory. A technically capable site is not enough if communication and documentation repeatedly delay decisions. A development partner may not be the right commercial supplier.

The manufacturer is part of the route to market, not a vendor added after the route is chosen.

## Pack configuration is commercial infrastructure

Packaging is where regulatory, manufacturing and channel requirements become physical.

A pack affects:

- label and leaflet requirements;
- production-line compatibility;
- transport and storage efficiency;
- case quantities;
- pharmacy handling;
- ordering behaviour;
- price perception;
- expiry and stock management.

A product can be technically correct and commercially awkward because the pack does not fit the market.

Pack decisions should be tested with buyers and operators before artwork and production assumptions become fixed.

## Price must reflect the complete route

The selling price has to support the full lifecycle, not just the first batch.

The model should include:

- development and transfer;
- regulatory fees;
- analytical and stability work;
- validation;
- manufacturing and packaging;
- quality oversight;
- storage and distribution;
- channel margin;
- inventory financing;
- expiry and write-off risk;
- commercial support;
- variations and maintenance.

The route may still be attractive. But the decision should be made with the complete cost visible.

This also changes negotiation. A company that understands the economics can discuss batch size, payment terms, packaging, capacity and price with a manufacturer as one commercial system rather than as isolated line items.

## Supply strategy belongs in launch planning

A launch forecast without a replenishment model is incomplete.

The company needs to know:

- manufacturing lead time;
- testing and release time;
- packaging-material lead time;
- shipping and receipt time;
- safety-stock requirement;
- reorder point;
- shelf-life exposure;
- alternate-source options;
- response to a delayed or failed batch.

This is especially important when launch volumes are uncertain.

Too little inventory damages service and buyer confidence. Too much inventory traps working capital and increases expiry risk. The correct position depends on lead time, demand variation and the consequence of a stock-out.

## The buyer needs a reason to adopt

A product does not enter a channel because the company has completed its internal tasks.

The buyer will assess the offer against an existing way of working. Price matters, but so do availability, ordering, service, account support, pack, payment terms and confidence in supply.

A credible launch plan should identify:

- the buying organisation;
- the decision-maker;
- the existing product or source;
- the reason to switch;
- the listing process;
- the information required;
- the expected order pattern;
- the service standard;
- the commercial terms.

Without this work, demand remains an assumption.

## Launch sequencing reduces avoidable risk

The route should be sequenced so that each commitment is supported by the previous decision.

A practical sequence may look like this:

1. Define the product and market thesis.
2. Confirm the regulatory pathway.
3. Test manufacturing and batch feasibility.
4. Build the full economics.
5. Validate pack and channel assumptions.
6. Establish the supply and quality route.
7. Secure development or sourcing commitments.
8. Prepare market-entry and account activity.
9. Place inventory against a realistic launch window.
10. Measure adoption and adjust replenishment.

The exact sequence changes by product, but the principle remains: do not let one function commit the company before the connected functions understand the consequence.

## The launch is a test of the design

A good launch is not created by adding energy at the end.

It is created by reducing contradiction at the beginning.

The product, pathway, manufacturer, pack, price, supply model and channel strategy should tell the same commercial story. When they do, launch execution becomes clearer. When they do not, the launch team inherits problems that communication cannot solve.

The route to market is designed before launch because every early decision becomes part of the market experience later.
