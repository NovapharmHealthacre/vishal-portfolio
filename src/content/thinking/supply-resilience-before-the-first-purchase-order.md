Pharmaceutical supply resilience is often discussed after a disruption: a manufacturer misses a batch, a material is delayed, a source market tightens or demand moves faster than the forecast.

By then, the company is working with decisions made months or years earlier.

The product may depend on one site. The pack may use a long-lead component. The minimum batch may make safety stock expensive. The quality agreement may leave responsibilities unclear. The forecast may never have been translated into a replenishment model.

Supply resilience therefore begins before the first purchase order. It begins when the product, manufacturer and operating model are chosen.

## Concentration risk starts with product design

A company can create single-source risk without realising it.

The risk may sit in:

- one active ingredient manufacturer;
- one finished-product site;
- one packaging line;
- one specialised component;
- one analytical laboratory;
- one source market;
- one release process;
- one logistics route.

Not every risk can be duplicated economically. The important step is to identify where concentration exists and understand the consequence.

A product with a simple process and several qualified suppliers may tolerate a leaner inventory position. A product with one specialist site, long lead times and limited shelf life requires a different supply strategy.

Resilience is not the absence of dependency. It is the deliberate management of dependency.

## Capacity is not the same as availability

A manufacturer may have the technical equipment to produce a product without having reliable capacity at the time the company needs it.

Commercial planning should ask:

- How far in advance is capacity reserved?
- What is the campaign frequency for the dosage form?
- Which products compete for the same line?
- How are small-volume products prioritised?
- What happens if a validation or commercial batch is delayed?
- Can the site support an urgent replacement batch?
- How does expected growth affect future slots?

These questions are especially important for a new product with uncertain demand. The manufacturer may prefer larger, less frequent campaigns while the market requires smaller and more flexible replenishment.

The supply model has to reconcile both realities.

## Lead time should be mapped, not quoted

A single lead-time number can hide several different clocks.

The complete replenishment cycle may include:

1. forecast confirmation;
2. purchase-order acceptance;
3. raw-material procurement;
4. packaging-material procurement;
5. production scheduling;
6. manufacturing;
7. testing;
8. batch release;
9. transport;
10. receipt and system availability.

Each stage can move independently.

A quoted twelve-week lead time may assume materials are already available. A packaging change may add months. A failed result may require investigation and retesting. International transport may be short compared with the time needed to schedule a new batch.

A resilient plan maps the stages, identifies the longest dependency and establishes the earliest point at which a problem can be detected.

## Batch size shapes the inventory strategy

Minimum batch size is one of the most important supply decisions because it connects manufacturing efficiency with market uncertainty.

A batch that is efficient for the manufacturer may create excessive inventory for the product owner. That inventory consumes cash, requires storage, reduces flexibility and increases expiry exposure.

A smaller batch may cost more per unit but allow more frequent replenishment and faster response to demand.

The right answer depends on:

- annual forecast;
- forecast confidence;
- shelf life;
- launch ramp;
- unit margin;
- production frequency;
- lead time;
- storage cost;
- cost of stock-out;
- cost of write-off.

The cheapest unit cost is not always the cheapest supply model.

## Quality agreements are operational documents

A quality agreement should do more than satisfy a checklist.

It should make responsibilities clear across manufacturing, testing, release, deviations, change control, complaints, recalls, documentation, audits and communication.

When a problem occurs, the speed and quality of the response depend on whether the parties already know:

- who assesses the event;
- who communicates with which authority;
- who controls the affected stock;
- who investigates the root cause;
- who approves corrective action;
- how customers are informed;
- which records are retained.

Ambiguity becomes delay at the moment when delay is most expensive.

The commercial team should understand these responsibilities because a quality event quickly becomes a supply and customer event.

## Warehouse and logistics planning should follow the product

Storage and transport design should reflect the product’s authorised conditions, pack, volume and service requirements.

The company needs to understand:

- temperature and handling conditions;
- case and pallet configuration;
- delivery frequency;
- order cut-off times;
- geographic service expectations;
- returns and quarantine processes;
- stock visibility;
- serial or batch traceability;
- recall capability.

A logistics provider can perform the physical work, but the company still needs an operating model that connects inventory, quality and customer decisions.

The warehouse is not simply a place where stock waits. It is part of the market-access route.

## Forecasting must translate into decisions

A forecast is only useful when it changes what the company does.

The supply plan should translate demand assumptions into:

- production timing;
- reorder points;
- safety stock;
- cash requirements;
- warehouse capacity;
- service targets;
- scenario actions.

It should also show the consequence of being wrong.

What happens if demand is half the forecast? What happens if it is double? What happens if a batch is delayed by eight weeks? What happens if the product has twelve months of remaining shelf life on arrival?

These scenarios are not pessimism. They are the information needed to decide how much flexibility the business can afford.

## Alternate sources require early investment

An alternate source is not created by adding a supplier name to a spreadsheet.

The alternative may require technical assessment, qualification, regulatory work, analytical transfer, packaging changes, quality agreements and commercial negotiation. The route can take significant time.

That means the decision to build an alternative should be based on the impact and probability of failure, not made after the primary source has already failed.

For some products, strategic inventory may be the more practical protection. For others, a second site or supplier is justified. In many cases, the best answer combines inventory, visibility and a prepared alternate route.

## Working capital is part of resilience

A company cannot hold resilient inventory without funding it.

Supply strategy therefore belongs inside the financial model.

Long lead times and large batches require cash before revenue. Slow channel adoption extends the cash cycle. Safety stock protects service but increases capital exposure. Payment terms with manufacturers and buyers can either support or destabilise the model.

The company should know how much cash is tied up at each stage and how that changes under different demand and delay scenarios.

A supply plan that the balance sheet cannot support is not resilient.

## A practical pre-order review

Before placing the first commercial purchase order, I would want the team to resolve:

- the complete lead-time map;
- production and release capacity;
- batch-size economics;
- shelf-life exposure;
- safety-stock logic;
- single-source dependencies;
- quality responsibilities;
- warehouse and transport readiness;
- demand scenarios;
- working-capital requirement;
- contingency actions.

The first order is not the beginning of supply strategy. It is the moment the earlier strategy becomes inventory.

Reliable pharmaceutical supply is designed before that moment, when the company still has the freedom to choose a different product, partner, batch, pack or route.
