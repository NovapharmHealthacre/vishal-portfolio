A pharmaceutical launch is decided long before the launch date. The route to market begins with product selection and is shaped by the regulatory pathway, manufacturer, batch size, pack, price, supply model and buyer proposition. By the time a team starts discussing a launch campaign, most of the decisive commercial choices have already been made.

My view is that route-to-market design should begin at the same time as product assessment. The objective is not to predict every detail years in advance. It is to identify the decisions that become expensive to reverse and resolve them before they harden into the programme.

## Product selection defines the route

Every product creates a different operating problem.

A straightforward oral solid with several qualified manufacturers may offer flexibility but face intense price competition. An oral liquid may address a clearer need while introducing formulation, packaging and minimum-batch challenges. A specialist product may have lower volume but require deeper technical and channel capability.

Before selecting the product, the company should understand:

- the patient and market need;
- current products and competitors;
- the legal and regulatory route;
- development complexity;
- manufacturing availability;
- likely pack and presentation;
- expected buying channel;
- indicative price and margin;
- supply concentration;
- working-capital requirement.

The route is not something applied to the product later. It is part of deciding whether the product belongs in the portfolio.

## The regulatory pathway affects the commercial clock

Different regulatory routes create different evidence, cost and timing requirements. Those differences change the commercial plan.

A programme that requires new development, bioequivalence, extensive analytical work or a long stability package has a different capital profile from one using an established legal basis. A variation-heavy product has a different lifecycle cost from a stable presentation. A route dependent on a reference product or source market carries different continuity risks.

The regulatory plan should therefore produce commercial answers:

- When can the product realistically launch?
- Which activities sit on the critical path?
- Which assumptions could add a year?
- Which markets can share development work?
- Which pack and artwork decisions must be made early?
- What ongoing maintenance cost should be built into the model?

A regulatory timeline is not merely a submission schedule. It is the operating clock of the investment.

## Manufacturer selection is market strategy

The manufacturer affects more than cost of goods.

The site determines available equipment, process scale, analytical support, documentation quality, inspection history, capacity, lead time, packaging options and willingness to support the product after approval.

A low quote can become expensive when the site cannot accommodate the launch batch, transfer the analytical method efficiently or produce the required pack. A technically excellent site can still be the wrong commercial fit if its batch size creates excessive inventory.

The manufacturer should be assessed against the intended market and portfolio, not just the first development activity.

Questions should include:

- Does the site understand the target regulatory standard?
- Can it support the dosage form and process risk?
- Is the minimum batch size compatible with demand?
- Can development, validation and commercial scale be sequenced sensibly?
- Are analytical, stability and packaging capabilities integrated?
- What documentation and change-control support will be available?
- Can the relationship scale across more than one product?

Choosing the manufacturer is one of the earliest route-to-market decisions.

## The pack is a commercial decision

Packaging is often treated as an artwork and compliance workstream. It is also part of the product proposition.

Bottle size, blister configuration, pack count, child resistance, dosing device, language, label space and case configuration influence manufacturing, logistics, pharmacy handling, patient use and price.

A pack that is familiar to the channel can reduce friction. A pack that is technically elegant but commercially unfamiliar can slow adoption. A pack size that improves unit economics may increase the buyer’s inventory burden.

The right presentation has to satisfy regulation, manufacturing and market behaviour together.

## Pricing begins with the full route

Price cannot be set responsibly from the manufacturer’s quotation alone.

The route may include:

- development and transfer cost;
- regulatory fees;
- packaging components and artwork;
- release and ongoing testing;
- freight and insurance;
- warehousing and distribution;
- quality and pharmacovigilance responsibilities;
- commercial discounts;
- financing and foreign-exchange exposure;
- expiry and returns;
- lifecycle variations and maintenance.

The model should show both unit economics and cash timing.

A product can appear profitable at steady state and still be difficult to fund during development and launch. It can show an attractive gross margin while tying up too much capital in one commercial batch.

Price is therefore the result of the route, not a number added at the end.

## The channel determines the launch sequence

The launch plan should be built around the way the product is bought.

A national wholesaler, pharmacy group, hospital channel and specialist distributor will each have different listing requirements, data needs, credit expectations and service models. The company needs to know which channel creates reach, which creates credibility, which creates margin and which creates operational complexity.

A sensible sequence may begin with a concentrated group of buyers that can provide useful demand information before wider distribution. Another product may need broad wholesale availability from the start. The answer depends on switching behaviour, clinical context, product availability and the role of the buyer.

The channel strategy should resolve:

- target accounts;
- listing process;
- commercial terms;
- minimum order and delivery model;
- product data;
- forecast ownership;
- launch inventory;
- replenishment;
- returns and customer support.

A product is not launched when it leaves the manufacturer. It is launched when the channel can order, receive, understand and replenish it.

## Demand should influence batch strategy

Forecasting is uncertain, especially for a new entrant. That does not make it optional.

The forecast connects the market to the factory.

It should be translated into:

- development and submission batch requirements;
- validation strategy;
- first commercial batch;
- safety stock;
- reorder point;
- lead-time coverage;
- expiry risk;
- cash requirement;
- upside and downside cases.

A forecast that exists only as annual sales volume is too abstract. The business needs a time-phased view that shows when inventory is made, released, delivered and paid for.

The most useful question is not “What is the forecast?” It is “Which decision changes if this forecast is wrong by half?”

## Launch readiness is cross-functional

A regulatory approval can arrive while the business is still unprepared to sell.

A practical launch-readiness review should cover:

- approved product and artwork;
- released commercial inventory;
- authorised supply route;
- quality agreements and procedures;
- pricing and commercial terms;
- customer master data;
- product information;
- ordering and invoicing;
- delivery and returns;
- complaint, defect and recall routes;
- demand and replenishment;
- ownership of each decision.

The review should expose dependencies rather than celebrate completed departments.

## The founder’s role

In an early pharmaceutical company, the founder often sits where the functions meet. That creates a responsibility to understand enough of each discipline to see when one decision is quietly weakening another.

The founder does not replace the regulatory, quality, technical or commercial specialist. The role is to make sure their work forms one route.

That means asking whether the selected product fits the company thesis, whether the manufacturer fits the market, whether the batch fits the forecast, whether the pack fits the channel and whether the commercial promise fits the operating capability.

## Design the route before the pressure

The closer a programme gets to launch, the harder it becomes to change fundamental assumptions. Timelines tighten, sunk cost grows and teams become attached to the plan.

Early route-to-market design creates room to stop, simplify or sequence differently.

It does not eliminate uncertainty. It identifies where uncertainty matters and who owns the next answer.

A strong pharmaceutical launch is not created by a final burst of activity. It is created by a series of aligned decisions made while there is still time to choose.
