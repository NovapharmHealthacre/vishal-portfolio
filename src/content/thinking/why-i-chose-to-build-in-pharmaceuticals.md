Pharmaceuticals attracted me because it is one of the few industries where commercial ambition has to survive scientific, regulatory, manufacturing and supply reality at the same time.

A product can look attractive on paper and still fail long before it reaches a patient. The regulatory route may be wrong. The manufacturer may not fit the dosage form or scale. The minimum batch may make the economics impossible. The pack may not suit the market. The buyer may have no reason to switch. Supply may depend on one fragile source.

That is why I chose to build here. The opportunity is not simply to sell a product. It is to design the route that makes a product commercially and operationally viable.

## The attraction of a difficult market

Easy markets invite speed. Difficult markets reward understanding.

In pharmaceuticals, the barriers are real. A company must understand the product, the permissions, the quality responsibilities, the manufacturing process, the supply chain and the commercial model. Each decision affects the next one. There is very little room for a strategy that looks impressive but cannot be executed.

I find that demanding rather than discouraging.

A difficult market creates room for operators who are prepared to learn the detail, build relationships across functions and stay with a problem long enough to understand where value is actually created. The work may take longer, but the knowledge compounds. A good decision about product selection or manufacturing can shape years of commercial performance.

## Why market access became central

Market access is often treated as the stage after approval. I see it differently.

The route to market begins when the product opportunity is first considered. Before committing capital, a company should understand whether the product can be registered, manufactured repeatedly, supplied reliably, priced sustainably and adopted by the channel.

Those are not separate questions. They form one commercial system.

If the regulatory pathway is viable but the batch size creates excessive inventory, the product may still fail. If the manufacturer can make the product but the pack is wrong for the market, the launch may still fail. If the price works but supply is unreliable, the relationship with buyers will not last.

The most interesting work sits in connecting those decisions before the company has already spent the money.

## Why the United Kingdom matters

The United Kingdom is a demanding and strategically important pharmaceutical market.

It has a mature regulatory system, a sophisticated healthcare and pharmacy infrastructure, established wholesale channels and a clear need for reliable access to medicines. It also sits outside the European Union’s regulatory framework, which means companies cannot treat UK and EU market entry as one undifferentiated plan.

That separation creates complexity, but it also creates opportunity for businesses that can understand product routes, packaging, licensing, supply and commercial sequencing across both systems.

NovaPharm was established in the UK because I want to build close to that market reality while maintaining strong links with manufacturers, technical partners and supply capabilities internationally.

## Why I founded NovaPharm

My pharmaceutical experience includes work with SyriMed between 2020 and 2025. Over that period, I became increasingly interested in the commercial architecture around medicines: how products are selected, how manufacturers are evaluated, how supply is secured and how a market-entry thesis becomes an operating company.

NovaPharm Healthcare was founded in 2025 to pursue that work with ownership.

The company’s direction is broader than a single product or transaction. It is being built around product and portfolio strategy, licensing pathways, manufacturing partnerships, technology transfer, sourcing, supply, distribution and commercialisation.

The goal is to create a company that can identify worthwhile pharmaceutical opportunities and build the route required to bring them into regulated markets responsibly and sustainably.

## What I want NovaPharm to become

I want NovaPharm to become a pharmaceutical company with the ability to connect five things well:

1. **Product judgement** — choosing opportunities with a credible clinical, regulatory and commercial case.
2. **Technical execution** — working with manufacturers and development partners that can support the product from transfer through commercial supply.
3. **Market access** — understanding the permissions, packaging, price and channel requirements before launch.
4. **Supply resilience** — building sourcing and operating structures that can withstand disruption and growth.
5. **Commercial discipline** — making sure the economics work across batch size, inventory, margin, working capital and demand.

Technology can strengthen that model, especially around product information, controlled documents, supplier qualification, inventory visibility and decision support. But the value of the technology depends on the quality of the underlying process.

## Why long-term thinking matters

Pharmaceutical entrepreneurship is not a fast route to visible success.

Regulatory timelines are long. Manufacturing decisions take time. Product development creates uncertainty. Partnerships depend on trust. Commercial adoption rarely follows a straight line.

That changes the founder’s job. The work is not to create constant announcements. It is to keep improving the quality of the position: a better product thesis, a stronger manufacturing route, clearer economics, a more dependable supply model and relationships that can support the next stage.

This type of progress is less visible than a consumer launch, but it is not less real.

## The kind of company I am trying to build

I am building for durability rather than novelty.

That means choosing products carefully, understanding the full cost of market entry, building partnerships that can survive difficult conversations and treating supply as a strategic capability rather than a purchasing function.

It also means accepting that the best commercial answer may sometimes be to stop a product early. Capital and attention should move towards opportunities where the regulatory route, manufacturer, market and economics reinforce one another.

The ambition is significant. So is the work required to earn it.

Pharmaceuticals is where I want to do that work because the problems are difficult, the decisions matter and the value of getting the route right extends beyond the company itself.

That is why I chose to build in pharmaceuticals. And that is why I am building NovaPharm Healthcare.
