Choosing a pharmaceutical CMO or CDMO is not a supplier comparison. It is a product-lifecycle decision.

The partner will influence development speed, regulatory quality, batch economics, launch timing, supply continuity and the company’s ability to manage change after approval. A low conversion price can be expensive if the site cannot support the dosage form, the target market or the commercial scale. A technically strong site can still be the wrong choice if its batch size, communication or business model does not fit the product.

The selection process should therefore test four kinds of fit: technical, regulatory, operational and commercial.

## Start with the exact product requirement

A manufacturer cannot be evaluated properly from a generic company profile.

The brief should define:

- dosage form and strength;
- formulation and process status;
- development work required;
- analytical methods and transfer needs;
- target markets;
- submission strategy;
- expected development, validation and commercial batches;
- proposed pack;
- annual forecast and growth scenario;
- required launch window;
- special containment or handling needs.

The clearer the requirement, the easier it becomes to distinguish capability from marketing language.

## Technical capability must match the process

A site may manufacture oral liquids without being suitable for every oral liquid.

The product may require suspension capability, controlled mixing, a specific filling range, nitrogen handling, high-potency controls, specialised cleaning, particular packaging equipment or a narrow environmental condition.

The evaluation should examine:

- equipment type and working range;
- minimum and maximum batch size;
- process similarity to existing products;
- analytical laboratory capability;
- method transfer and validation experience;
- cleaning and cross-contamination controls;
- packaging-line compatibility;
- utilities and environmental controls;
- technical team availability;
- scale-up approach.

The important question is not whether the site has equipment. It is whether the product can move through that equipment with a controlled and commercially realistic process.

## Regulatory history should be read in context

Approvals and inspection history matter because the target market determines the expected standard and documentation.

The company should review:

- current manufacturing authorisations;
- recent inspection outcomes;
- scope of authorised dosage forms;
- markets currently supplied;
- experience supporting submissions in the intended region;
- history of significant observations or restrictions;
- quality-system maturity;
- ability to support audits and due diligence.

A certificate alone does not answer every question. The scope, site, dosage form and current status have to match the proposed work.

The most useful evidence is current, site-specific and connected to the activity being discussed.

## Documentation quality predicts execution quality

Regulated-market projects generate a large volume of documents: development reports, specifications, methods, protocols, batch records, stability data, validation documents, change controls and responses to regulatory questions.

The manufacturer’s ability to produce accurate, timely and internally consistent documentation can determine the submission timeline as much as the manufacturing work itself.

During due diligence, assess:

- sample documents and templates;
- review and approval workflows;
- response time for technical questions;
- version control;
- data integrity practices;
- ownership of source documents;
- language and translation needs;
- ability to support deficiency responses;
- archival and retrieval.

A project can lose months through documentation that is technically complete but difficult to review, reconcile or defend.

## Batch size is a commercial filter

Minimum batch size should be discussed before the company becomes attached to the site.

The manufacturer may optimise for equipment utilisation while the product owner needs launch flexibility. A commercial minimum that produces several years of stock can create expiry risk, working-capital pressure and a false unit-cost advantage.

Evaluate separate requirements for:

- laboratory and development batches;
- engineering or process-optimisation batches;
- submission batches;
- process-validation batches;
- first commercial batches;
- mature commercial scale.

The ideal partner may be able to support a smaller early lifecycle and increase scale as demand becomes clearer.

## Technology transfer is a relationship test

Technology transfer reveals how the two organisations work together.

A strong partner will want to understand the formulation, process history, critical parameters, analytical methods, known risks, packaging, stability and intended commercial model. It will identify gaps early and define who owns each action.

The transfer plan should cover:

- knowledge package;
- gap assessment;
- responsibilities;
- method transfer;
- equipment and process fit;
- material sourcing;
- trial and engineering work;
- stability strategy;
- validation;
- change control;
- reporting and decision governance.

A site that treats transfer as document receipt rather than knowledge movement is likely to create problems later.

## Communication is an operating capability

Pharmaceutical projects depend on decisions across technical, quality, regulatory and commercial teams. Communication quality affects whether issues are identified early or discovered at the next milestone.

Look for:

- one accountable project lead;
- clear meeting rhythm;
- written action tracking;
- realistic escalation paths;
- direct access to technical and quality specialists;
- transparent timing and capacity discussions;
- willingness to communicate bad news early.

Responsiveness before contract signature is not enough. The company should understand how the site manages projects when several customers compete for attention.

## Commercial fit goes beyond price

The quotation should make the complete commercial structure visible.

Review:

- development and transfer fees;
- analytical and stability charges;
- batch and validation costs;
- packaging and component mark-up;
- regulatory-support fees;
- annual maintenance charges;
- minimum order quantities;
- payment terms;
- price-review mechanisms;
- capacity reservation;
- write-off responsibility;
- termination and technology-return provisions.

A low headline manufacturing price can be offset by high fixed charges, inflexible batches or expensive lifecycle support.

The best commercial agreement aligns incentives. The manufacturer should have a credible reason to support the product before and after launch.

## Plan for the lifecycle, not only the submission

The partner selected for development may become the commercial source for many years.

The company should consider:

- expected volume growth;
- future pack or strength extensions;
- capacity expansion;
- alternate-site strategy;
- change management;
- ongoing stability;
- regulatory variations;
- product complaints and recalls;
- business continuity;
- exit and transfer rights.

A site that is ideal for the first submission batch may not be ideal for commercial supply. That tension should be resolved deliberately rather than discovered after approval.

## A practical selection scorecard

I would assess a CMO across eight areas:

1. Technical fit
2. Regulatory and quality fit
3. Documentation capability
4. Batch-size and scale fit
5. Transfer capability
6. Project communication
7. Commercial structure
8. Lifecycle and supply fit

The score should not replace judgement. It should make the trade-offs visible.

The right CMO is not necessarily the largest, cheapest or most heavily certified. It is the partner whose operating model fits the product, market, scale and stage of the company.

That fit is what turns a manufacturing contract into a viable route to market.
