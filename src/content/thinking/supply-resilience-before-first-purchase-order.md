Pharmaceutical supply resilience begins before the first purchase order. It is created when the product is selected, the manufacturer is qualified, the batch strategy is agreed, lead times are understood and alternative routes are designed. Once a shortage has started, most of the important choices are already constrained.

A resilient supply model is not the same as holding excessive inventory. It is the ability to see risk early, understand where the chain can fail and recover without losing control of product quality or market continuity.

## Start with concentration risk

Every supply chain contains concentration.

A product may depend on one active ingredient source, one manufacturing site, one analytical laboratory, one packaging component, one qualified warehouse or one freight route. Concentration is not automatically unacceptable. It becomes dangerous when the company does not know it exists or has no realistic response.

The first step is to map the chain at the level where failure would stop supply:

- active and critical excipient sources;
- manufacturing and packaging sites;
- testing and release functions;
- printed and specialist components;
- storage and transport;
- import and wholesale responsibilities;
- market-specific artwork or packs;
- regulatory dependencies.

The map should distinguish a theoretical alternative from a qualified one. A second supplier listed in a spreadsheet does not create resilience if the material has not been approved, the site is outside the dossier or the lead time makes recovery impossible.

## Capacity is more than annual volume

A manufacturer may have enough total capacity and still be unable to support the product when needed.

The relevant questions include:

- which equipment train is required;
- how often the product can be scheduled;
- campaign and cleaning constraints;
- minimum batch size;
- normal and surge lead time;
- changeover and packaging capacity;
- analytical-release timing;
- maintenance shutdowns;
- competing products;
- material-ordering windows.

Capacity should be understood as available production slots inside the required lead time, not simply litres or tablets per year.

This matters particularly for smaller products. A manufacturer may be technically capable but commercially reluctant to interrupt a large campaign for a low-volume order. The relationship between forecast, batch size and scheduling has to be clear before launch.

## Lead time should be decomposed

A single quoted lead time hides too much.

The true replenishment cycle may include:

1. forecast confirmation;
2. purchase-order acceptance;
3. material procurement;
4. component and artwork production;
5. manufacturing slot;
6. testing;
7. release;
8. export documentation;
9. freight;
10. import or receipt;
11. warehouse release;
12. delivery to the channel.

Each stage has different variability and ownership.

A twelve-week manufacturing lead time can become twenty weeks when printed components, testing or freight are added. A delay in one market-specific item can hold the entire batch.

The company should know which stages can run in parallel, which sit on the critical path and which assumptions are based on best-case performance.

## Batch size creates both protection and risk

Larger batches can improve unit economics and reduce the frequency of manufacturing. They can also create inventory concentration, expiry exposure and a larger cash requirement.

Smaller batches can improve flexibility but increase conversion cost, testing cost and scheduling difficulty.

The right batch strategy balances:

- expected demand;
- demand volatility;
- shelf life;
- manufacturing and release lead time;
- minimum economic batch;
- storage cost;
- working capital;
- shortage impact;
- ability to make another batch;
- supplier willingness.

The answer may change over the product lifecycle. A cautious launch batch can provide market information. A later scale-up can improve economics once replenishment behaviour is understood.

Resilience is not maximising inventory. It is choosing an inventory position that reflects the real recovery time.

## Quality agreements are operating tools

A quality agreement should do more than satisfy a document checklist.

It should define who is responsible for:

- supplier and material approval;
- manufacturing and testing;
- batch certification or release;
- change control;
- deviations and investigations;
- complaints;
- product defects and recalls;
- stability commitments;
- document retention;
- audits;
- transport;
- subcontractors;
- regulatory communication.

These responsibilities become critical under pressure.

When a deviation threatens supply, the business needs to know who investigates, who assesses impact, who communicates and who decides. Ambiguity adds delay and can create inconsistent action.

A resilient relationship is built on clear responsibilities before the first problem arrives.

## Forecasting should show uncertainty

A single forecast number encourages false confidence.

The useful model has at least a base, downside and upside case. It shows the timing of demand, not only annual volume. It connects the market forecast to inventory, reorder point, batch schedule and cash.

The company should identify which variables matter most:

- listing date;
- buyer adoption;
- competitor availability;
- price movement;
- tender timing;
- seasonality;
- supply interruption elsewhere;
- pack or product change;
- customer concentration.

A forecast should not pretend to remove uncertainty. It should show what the business will do under different outcomes.

## Warehousing and logistics must fit the product

The distribution model should be designed around product requirements and customer expectations.

Questions include:

- storage conditions;
- controlled-temperature needs;
- batch and expiry traceability;
- quarantine and release status;
- returns;
- recall execution;
- delivery cut-off and service level;
- split-case or unit handling;
- geographic coverage;
- import and customs processes;
- contingency transport;
- data exchange.

The cheapest warehouse or carrier may not be the lowest-risk route when product handling, responsiveness and quality oversight are considered.

Outsourcing the physical activity does not outsource the responsibility to understand it.

## Alternative routes need regulatory reality

A second manufacturer, warehouse or supplier can strengthen resilience only when it can actually be used.

That may require:

- technical transfer;
- regulatory variation;
- qualification batches;
- method transfer;
- new quality agreements;
- artwork or packaging changes;
- supplier approval;
- system and master-data changes;
- inventory transition.

The time and cost of activating the alternative should be known.

Some alternatives are worth qualifying before launch. Others can be developed as part of the lifecycle. The choice depends on the clinical importance of the product, concentration risk, lead time and economics.

## Governance turns information into action

A resilient supply model needs a regular decision process.

A practical review can cover:

- demand versus forecast;
- inventory by status, batch and expiry;
- open orders;
- manufacturing and release progress;
- material and component risk;
- deviations and changes;
- supplier performance;
- upcoming regulatory events;
- cash exposure;
- contingency actions.

The meeting is not the control. The decisions and follow-through are.

Each material risk should have an owner, an action, a due date and a clear trigger for escalation.

## Resilience is designed, then maintained

Supply resilience is never finished.

Products change. Manufacturers reprioritise. Demand moves. Regulations evolve. Freight routes and currency shift. A chain that was acceptable at launch may become fragile at scale.

The operating model has to keep testing its assumptions.

The best time to discover that a product depends on one difficult component is before the first commercial order. The best time to understand replenishment is before inventory falls. The best time to agree responsibilities is before a deviation.

A purchase order starts production. It does not create resilience.

Resilience comes from the work that made the order safe to place.
