An earlier version of this page told a clean story: technology first, pharmaceuticals later, with one decisive turn between them.

It was dramatic. It was also too simple.

My pharmaceutical experience predates NovaPharm Healthcare. It includes work with SyriMed between 2020 and 2025. The surviving evidence does not support the old claim that I entered the sector with no previous pharmaceutical background, and I will not keep a better story at the expense of a truer one.

This is the correction.

## Why neat founder stories are dangerous

Founder biographies tend to compress. Several years become a moment. A body of work becomes an epiphany. Uncertainty disappears because a finished story sounds more convincing when every earlier decision points towards the company that exists today.

Real work is rarely that clean.

People learn through overlapping roles, informal exposure, projects that do not fit a title and long periods when the eventual direction is not obvious. Turning that into a heroic pivot may make the page easier to read. It can also erase the experience that actually shaped the decision.

The old version leaned on contrast: fast technology, slow pharmaceuticals; naïve entrant, specialist field; one world abandoned for another. Contrast is useful in an essay. It is not evidence.

## What the record supports

The public-safe record is narrower:

- My pharmaceutical experience predates NovaPharm.
- It includes work with SyriMed between 2020 and 2025.
- NovaPharm Healthcare Ltd was incorporated on 15 September 2025.
- I am its founder and chief executive.
- The company's regulated operating capabilities remain under development.

The record does not support inventing a job title for the SyriMed work or publishing confidential responsibilities. It does not support turning planning into a licence, a customer or an operational capability.

That is enough.

## The real transition

The useful transition was not from one industry label to another. It was from speed as the main measure of progress to evidence as the main measure of progress.

In a fast environment, visible output can dominate. A launch, a dashboard or a growth curve gives the organisation a shared signal. In a regulated environment, progress is often quieter: a requirement understood correctly, a responsibility assigned, a supplier checked, a document controlled, a claim made narrower because the evidence is not yet there.

The second form can feel slower. It is also harder to fake.

That is the work I chose to keep following: access, licensing and supply questions in markets where the quality of the process matters as much as the ambition of the outcome.

## What I would keep from the old essay

I would keep its impatience with borrowed status.

I would keep the belief that difficult work can become an advantage when most people optimise for immediate recognition.

I would keep the preference for depth over noise, and for an operating system over a personal brand.

But I would remove the claims that make the journey look cleaner, faster or more complete than it was. The lesson is stronger without them.

## Why this page remains

Deleting the old route would be easy. Keeping it as a correction is more useful.

A public record should be allowed to improve. When evidence conflicts with a biography, the biography should change. When a capability is still planned, the language should say so. When a fact is private and unnecessary, it should stay out.

Credibility is not the absence of revision. It is the willingness to revise before a contradiction becomes a defence.

The story is less dramatic now.

It is also true enough to build on.
