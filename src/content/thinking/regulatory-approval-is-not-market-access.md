Regulatory approval answers an essential question: may this product be placed on the market under the approved conditions?

Market access asks a larger one: can the product reach the market repeatedly, at a sustainable price, through a channel that will adopt it, with supply that can be maintained?

The distinction matters because pharmaceutical companies often organise these questions in sequence. Regulatory works on approval. Technical works on manufacturing. Commercial works on price and launch. Supply works on availability. The market-access problem appears at the end, when the decisions are already difficult to change.

A stronger approach is to connect six questions from the beginning.

## 1. Can the product be approved?

The regulatory pathway determines the evidence, timelines, legal responsibilities and product presentation required for market entry.

The first task is not simply to identify a molecule. It is to understand the exact product opportunity: dosage form, strength, indication, reference product, source market, development route, data requirements and lifecycle obligations.

A pathway that is technically available may still be commercially unattractive. The evidence burden may be too high, the timeline too long or the post-approval maintenance too expensive for the expected market.

The regulatory route therefore belongs inside the commercial decision, not outside it.

## 2. Can it be manufactured repeatedly?

A development batch proves very little if the product cannot be transferred, validated and produced economically at commercial scale.

The manufacturer needs the right equipment, analytical capability, quality systems, batch-size range and experience with the dosage form. The process must fit the site. The packaging line must fit the presentation. The documentation must support the intended market.

This is where many attractive product ideas begin to weaken.

A manufacturer may be technically capable but commercially unsuitable because the minimum batch size creates too much inventory. Another may offer a lower conversion cost but lack the documentation quality required for a regulated-market submission. A third may be strong at development but unable to reserve commercial capacity when demand grows.

Manufacturing viability is not a procurement question. It is a market-access question.

## 3. Can it be supplied compliantly?

Approval and manufacturing do not create supply on their own.

The company needs a clear route for sourcing, release, storage, transport, inventory, complaints, defects and recall. Each hand-off needs an accountable organisation, appropriate permissions and controlled documentation.

Supply risk also has a commercial dimension. A product dependent on one source, one packaging site or one long-lead component may be vulnerable even when every participant is individually capable.

The practical questions include:

- How long does replenishment take?
- What inventory is required to cover the lead time?
- Which materials have single-source risk?
- How will demand variability affect stock?
- What is the contingency if a batch fails or a site is delayed?
- Can the working-capital model support the required inventory?

A product that cannot be supplied reliably does not have durable market access.

## 4. Can it be priced sustainably?

Price must cover more than manufacturing cost.

The full economics include development, regulatory fees, stability, validation, packaging, quality oversight, logistics, warehousing, inventory financing, write-off risk, channel margin, commercial support and lifecycle maintenance.

A product can appear attractive when the calculation begins with the purchase price and ends with the selling price. The margin often changes when the full route is included.

The right question is not whether the product can be sold at a margin once. It is whether the margin can support the product through normal variation in demand, supply cost, lead time and channel expectations.

That is especially important for specialist medicines, where volumes may be smaller and supply continuity more important than headline scale.

## 5. Can the channel adopt it?

A launch plan is incomplete until the buyer has a reason to change behaviour.

Wholesalers, pharmacy groups, hospitals and other channels assess more than price. They care about availability, service level, pack configuration, ordering process, account support, payment terms, product information and confidence in continued supply.

The manufacturer’s preferred pack may not be the buyer’s preferred pack. The company’s forecast may not match the channel’s ordering pattern. A theoretical saving may not justify the operational cost of switching.

Channel adoption should influence product and launch design early.

A company needs to understand:

- who makes the purchasing decision;
- how the product is currently sourced;
- what would cause the buyer to switch;
- what data or assurance the buyer requires;
- how the product will be listed, ordered and replenished;
- which commercial terms are realistic.

Without adoption, approval remains inventory.

## 6. Can patients access it reliably?

The final measure of market access is not the regulatory milestone or the first purchase order. It is whether the product remains available through the healthcare system when it is needed.

That depends on the entire route: correct product selection, appropriate approval, dependable manufacturing, compliant distribution, sustainable economics and channel adoption.

A weak decision at any earlier stage eventually appears as an access problem.

This is why market access is not one department. It is the operating logic connecting the company’s technical and commercial decisions.

## A practical decision framework

Before committing significant capital, I would want a product team to answer six questions in one document:

1. **Approval:** What is the exact regulatory route, evidence burden and timeline?
2. **Manufacturing:** Which site can make the product repeatedly at the required quality and scale?
3. **Supply:** How will the product move through an authorised and resilient supply chain?
4. **Economics:** Does the full lifecycle margin justify the capital and risk?
5. **Channel:** Who will buy it, why will they switch and how will they order it?
6. **Access:** What conditions must remain true for the product to stay available?

The value of the framework is not the six headings. It is the requirement to resolve them together.

## The commercial implication

A product should not advance because one function has a strong answer.

It should advance when the regulatory, technical, supply and commercial answers reinforce each other.

That may lead to fewer products entering development. It should also lead to fewer expensive surprises after approval.

Regulatory approval is indispensable. But it is not the destination.

The destination is a product that can be manufactured, supplied, adopted and accessed repeatedly. That is market access.
