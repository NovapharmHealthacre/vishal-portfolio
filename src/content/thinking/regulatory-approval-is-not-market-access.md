Regulatory approval is essential, but it is not the same as market access. Approval answers whether a medicine may be placed on the market. Access depends on whether the product can also be manufactured consistently, supplied through the correct route, priced sustainably, adopted by the channel and made reliably available to the people who need it.

When those questions are handled in sequence rather than together, a company can spend years obtaining an approval for a product that is difficult to launch. My operating view is simple: the route to market should be designed while the product strategy is being designed, not after the regulatory work is complete.

## The six questions behind access

I use six connected questions when assessing a pharmaceutical opportunity:

1. Can it be approved?
2. Can it be manufactured repeatedly?
3. Can it be supplied compliantly?
4. Can it be priced sustainably?
5. Can the channel adopt it?
6. Can patients access it reliably?

No single answer is enough.

A positive regulatory assessment cannot compensate for a manufacturing process that does not fit the selected site. A favourable cost of goods is not useful if the minimum batch size creates years of inventory. A technically correct pack can still be wrong for the buyer. A product that reaches the channel once but cannot be replenished has not achieved dependable access.

The value lies in resolving the questions together.

## Approval defines permission, not demand

A marketing authorisation establishes the legal basis for placing a medicine on the market under its approved conditions. It does not create a customer, a forecast or a channel strategy.

The commercial case still has to be made.

That means understanding the existing treatment and supply landscape, the number and behaviour of competitors, the buying route, the reference price, expected margin, substitution dynamics, pack preferences and the reason a buyer would change supplier.

In specialist medicines, the market may be smaller but the operational importance of continuity can be higher. In a crowded generic market, the addressable volume may look attractive while price pressure makes the opportunity weak. In either case, approval is the beginning of the commercial test, not the end.

## Manufacturing can change the product strategy

A product may be scientifically straightforward and operationally difficult.

The formulation may require equipment the preferred manufacturer does not have. The analytical method may be difficult to transfer. The stability programme may extend the timeline. The packaging line may not support the required presentation. The batch size may be incompatible with the launch forecast.

These are not manufacturing details to resolve later. They shape the investment decision.

A founder should understand early:

- the proposed manufacturing process;
- critical material and process risks;
- equipment and scale compatibility;
- analytical-method requirements;
- development, submission and validation batch strategy;
- packaging configuration;
- expected yields and losses;
- minimum and economic batch sizes;
- lead times;
- lifecycle capacity.

The question is not merely whether a site can make the product. It is whether that site can make the right product, in the right pack, at the right scale, with documentation suitable for the intended market.

## Supply requires an authorised chain

Pharmaceutical supply is a chain of responsibilities, not a transport booking.

The legal entity, product, manufacturing site, importer, wholesaler, warehouse, carrier and customer route all have to fit the applicable permissions and quality system. Good Distribution Practice extends beyond temperature. It covers the integrity of the authorised supply chain, storage, transportation, records, recall readiness and oversight of outsourced activities.

A product opportunity therefore needs a supply architecture.

That architecture should identify:

- who owns each regulated responsibility;
- where title and physical possession change;
- which supplier and site approvals are required;
- which quality agreements are needed;
- how batch and product records remain traceable;
- how deviations, complaints, defects and recalls are handled;
- what happens when the primary route fails.

A fragile supply route can turn a commercially attractive product into an unreliable one.

## Price must survive the full cost of access

A purchase price or manufacturing quote is not the complete cost of a pharmaceutical product.

The economic model may also include development, analytical transfer, stability, regulatory fees, artwork, packaging components, release testing, quality oversight, freight, warehousing, insurance, financing, inventory expiry, returns, commercial discounts and ongoing lifecycle maintenance.

The timing of cash matters as much as the headline margin.

A business may pay for development and registration long before revenue begins. It may fund manufacturing months before the product is sold. A large batch can improve unit cost while increasing working-capital exposure and expiry risk.

Sustainable pricing therefore requires a cash-flow view, not only a gross-margin calculation.

## The channel needs a reason to adopt

A product does not enter a market in the abstract. It enters through a channel.

That channel may include wholesalers, pharmacy groups, hospitals, specialist distributors, online platforms or other approved buyers. Each has its own listing process, data requirements, commercial expectations, service levels and switching behaviour.

The pack, lead time, minimum order, credit terms, delivery model and product information all affect adoption.

The key question is: why should this channel allocate attention, inventory and working capital to the product?

The answer may be reliable availability, a differentiated pack, improved economics, stronger service, a shortage response or access to a product that is difficult to source. Without a clear answer, launch planning becomes a hope that approval will create its own demand.

## Patient access is the final operating test

The purpose of the route is not to complete a sequence of internal milestones. It is to make the product available reliably.

That means avoiding a launch that cannot be replenished. It means designing enough visibility to identify supply risk early. It means understanding whether the product can remain viable when input cost, exchange rates, demand or lead time changes.

Patient access is affected by decisions made months or years earlier: manufacturer choice, batch size, safety stock, pack presentation, channel coverage and the quality of the demand assumptions.

The final outcome is therefore built into the first decisions.

## A practical decision framework

Before committing significant capital, I would expect an opportunity to have one integrated assessment covering:

### Product and regulatory

What is the precise product opportunity, intended market, reference product or legal basis, evidence requirement and expected regulatory sequence?

### Technical and manufacturing

Which site, process, equipment, methods, batches, packaging components and validation activities are required?

### Supply and quality

Which authorised entities and sites perform each step, how is oversight maintained and what alternative route exists if the first route fails?

### Commercial and channel

Who buys the product, why would they adopt it, what service level is expected and what launch sequence fits the channel?

### Economics and capital

What is the full lifecycle cost, how much working capital is required and what assumptions most affect viability?

### Access and continuity

Can the product be made available repeatedly, not merely launched once?

## The commercial implication

Companies often discover market-access problems after regulatory and development spending has already been committed. By then, changing the manufacturer, pack, batch size or route to market can be slow and expensive.

The better approach is to make market access the operating logic of the programme from the beginning.

Regulatory approval remains a non-negotiable milestone. It should be pursued inside a wider commercial and supply design.

A product reaches the market only when permission, manufacturing, supply, economics and channel execution work as one system. That is the difference between an approved product and accessible medicine.
